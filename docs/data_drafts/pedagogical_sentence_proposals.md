# Pedagogical Sentence Proposals — Batch A (Ranks 1–5)

**Author:** Educational Publisher  
**Purpose:** Draft 5 new sentences targeting the top-5 structural gaps from the aligned expansion strategy  
**Audience:** Intermediate learners (HSK 3–5)

---

## Sentence 1: BEI + Resultative Complement

**Gap:** Rank 1 — No current BEI sentence pairs the passive with a resultative complement chain.

**Chinese:** 他被老板骂得不敢说话。  
**Pinyin:** Tā bèi lǎobǎn mà de bù gǎn shuō huà.  
**Translation:** He was scolded by the boss so badly that he didn't dare speak.

**Explanation:**
This sentence layers two key structures: a **被 passive** and a **degree complement**. The topic 他 is the person affected, and 被 introduces the agent 老板. The verb 骂 ("scold") then leads into a 得-complement that describes *how badly* it went: 不敢说话 ("didn't dare speak"). In the tree, you'll see the complement clause nesting inside the passive frame — the 得 opens a mini-sentence that describes the consequence of the scolding. This is the kind of sentence where the tree is essential: the linear word order hides three levels of structure (passive → verb → result description), and the tree peels them apart.

## Sentence 2: 连 with Clausal Scope

**Gap:** Rank 2 — Current 连 examples only wrap noun phrases; none wrap an entire clause.

**Chinese:** 她连他叫什么名字都不记得了。  
**Pinyin:** Tā lián tā jiào shénme míngzi dōu bú jìde le.  
**Translation:** She can't even remember what his name is.

**Explanation:**
Most 连…都 sentences emphasize a single noun: "even children know." But here, the 连 bracket wraps an **entire embedded clause**: 他叫什么名字 ("what his name is"). The tree shows this dramatically — the 连 node contains a full topic-comment structure inside it, with its own subject (他), verb (叫), and question object (什么名字). The 都 then reinforces the emphasis from outside the clause. This is the strongest demonstration of recursive nesting in the dataset: a sentence inside a sentence, all wrapped in an emphasis marker. If learners ever wondered whether Chinese sentences can nest, this is the proof.

## Sentence 3: Double Topic + 是…的 Focus

**Gap:** Rank 3 — No sentence combines a double-topic frame with a 是…的 cleft structure.

**Chinese:** 那本书，作者是在北京写的。  
**Pinyin:** Nà běn shū, zuòzhě shì zài Běijīng xiě de.  
**Translation:** That book — the author wrote it in Beijing.

**Explanation:**
This sentence stacks two patterns you've already seen separately. The outer **Topic** is 那本书 ("that book"), setting the big-picture frame. The inner **Topic** is 作者 ("the author"), zooming in on a specific aspect. Then the comment uses **是…的** to spotlight *where* the writing happened: 在北京 ("in Beijing"). The tree reveals depth-4 nesting: Sentence → Topic (那本书) → Comment → Topic (作者) → Comment (是在北京写的). Without the tree, you'd see a flat line of characters; with it, you see a Russian-doll structure where each layer narrows the focus.

## Sentence 4: Compound Directional Potential

**Gap:** Rank 4 — Current potential complements only cover simple V-不-R; none show the compound directional stack.

**Chinese:** 这个箱子太重了，我一个人搬不上去。  
**Pinyin:** Zhège xiāngzi tài zhòng le, wǒ yí ge rén bān bú shàng qù.  
**Translation:** This box is too heavy — I can't carry it upstairs by myself.

**Explanation:**
Potential complements tell you whether an action *can* reach its result. The interesting part here is the **three-layer complement stack**: the verb 搬 ("carry"), then 不 ("cannot"), then the direction 上 ("up"), then the further direction 去 ("away from speaker"). In the tree, you'll see: Head Verb (搬) → Particle (不) → Complement (上) → Complement (去). Each layer adds meaning: "carry — cannot — upward — away." This is the kind of dense, compact structure that makes Mandarin feel impenetrable in linear text, but perfectly logical when you can see the layers stacked in the tree.

## Sentence 5: BEI Passive with Agentless Retained Object

**Gap:** Rank 5 — No current BEI sentence shows a "retained object" where the patient stays post-verbal instead of becoming the topic.

**Chinese:** 我昨天被偷了一个手机。  
**Pinyin:** Wǒ zuótiān bèi tōu le yí ge shǒujī.  
**Translation:** Yesterday I had a phone stolen (from me).

**Explanation:**
This is a classic exam pitfall. In most 被 sentences, the thing affected sits at the front as the Topic: "The phone 被 stolen." But here, **the person** (我) is the topic, and the thing stolen (手机) stays *after* the verb as a retained object. The tree makes the structural surprise visible: 我 sits in the Topic position, 被 opens an agentless passive (no one is named as the thief), and 手机 appears inside the VP as the object of 偷. Learners who expect the 被-topic to always be the patient will see from the tree that 我 is actually the *experiencer* — the person something bad happened to — not the thing that was stolen.
