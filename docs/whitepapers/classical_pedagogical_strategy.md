# Classical Pedagogical Strategy

**Author:** Educational Publisher  
**Purpose:** Counter-recommendation to `classical_expansion_strategy.md`, evaluating the 6 structural gaps and source diversity plan from a learner's perspective  
**Constraint:** Prioritize structures where the visual AST tree model is *particularly useful* for explaining the grammar

---

## Executive Summary

The Linguistics Specialist's analysis is thorough and well-reasoned. The structural gaps are real, the source diversity concern is valid, and the cross-dataset resonance principle is exactly right. My counter-recommendation **agrees with the overall direction** but proposes three adjustments:

1. **Phase the rollout**: 13 sentences at once is too large to QA, translate, and explain well. Split into Phase A (8 sentences) and Phase B (5 sentences).
2. **Reorder priorities**: Conditional (若/則) should be Rank 1, not Rank 2 — it's the most frequently encountered pattern in any classical text a learner will read.
3. **Drop one proposed category**: The Passive (為…所…) category, while structurally elegant, should be **deferred** to Phase B. It's the least frequent of the three new patterns and the most confusing for intermediate learners who are still absorbing the modern 被 system.

## Gap-by-Gap Pedagogical Evaluation

### Gap S1: Classical Conditionals (若/苟/則) — **STRONG AGREE, UPGRADE TO RANK 1**

| Linguist Rating | Pedagogical Rating | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| ★★★★★ (Rank 2) | ★★★★★ (Rank 1) | ✅ **UPGRADE** |

**Rationale:** Conditionals are the single most common complex structure in classical Chinese. A learner opening *any* classical text will encounter 若…則… or 苟…則… within the first paragraph. The modern dataset already has 8 conditional sentences (如果…就…), so adding the classical ancestor creates an immediate cross-dataset "aha!" moment.

**Agree with 3 sentences.** Specific recommendations:
- 若/如 conditional — **must be from 《莊子》** (their conditional parables are iconic)
- 苟 conditional — good candidate for 《左傳》 or 《戰國策》 (political rhetoric)
- 使/令 counterfactual — **recategorize**: this is actually a causative-conditional hybrid. Consider placing it in the Causative category instead to avoid category bloat.

| Linguist Rating | Pedagogical Rating | Verdict |

### Gap S3: Passive (為…所…/見) — ⚠️ **DEFER TO PHASE B**

| Linguist Rating | Pedagogical Rating | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| ★★★★★ (Rank 3) | ★★★☆☆ | ⚠️ **DEFER** |

**Rationale:** While structurally fascinating and historically important, the classical passive presents a **pedagogical timing problem**:

1. Our target learners are still actively learning the modern 被 passive system. Adding 為…所… and 見-passive simultaneously creates **three competing passive frameworks** (被, 為…所…, 見) before the first one is solidified.
2. The 見-passive is extremely rare outside pre-Han texts. It's linguistically important but practically marginal for our HSK-adjacent audience.
3. The 為…所… pattern, while beautiful, requires learners to understand 所 as a nominalizer first — which means Gap S6 (Nominalizer expansion) should come *before* the passive, not after.

**Recommendation:** Defer to Phase B. When we do add it, start with **為…所… only** (1 sentence) and defer 見-passive to a future "Advanced Classical" expansion.

### Gap S4: Topic-Comment in Classical — **AGREE**

| Linguist Rating | Pedagogical Rating | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| ★★★★ (Rank 6) | ★★★★☆ | ✅ **AGREE, upgrade slightly** |

**Rationale:** The zero-copula pattern (X者，Y也) is **the single most important pattern for reading classical Chinese**. It appears in every philosophical text, every definition, every categorical statement. The fact that we only have 2 examples is a real gap.

**Agree with 2 sentences.** The contrastive topic pair (X者Y也，非Z也) is especially valuable because it uses 非 from the Negation category, creating cross-category reinforcement.

**Source:** Agree with 《詩經》 for one — but only if the chosen verse is simple and well-known. 《詩經》 vocabulary can be extremely archaic. Recommend a widely anthologized verse like 關關雎鳩 or 蒹葭蒼蒼.

### Gap S6: Nominalizers (者/所) — **AGREE, SEQUENCE BEFORE PASSIVE**

| Linguist Rating | Pedagogical Rating | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| ★★★★ (Rank 5) | ★★★★ | ✅ **AGREE** |

**Rationale:** The 之所以 construction is essential — learners encounter it in modern Chinese too (albeit fossilized). And 者 in conditional/generic sense ("whoever…", "if one…") is frequent enough to justify its own example.

**Critical sequencing note:** These **must** be added in Phase A, before the Passive category (Phase B). The 為…所… passive relies on understanding 所 as a nominalizer. If learners see 所 as a nominalizer first, the passive pattern clicks instantly.

## Key Pedagogical Principles Applied

1. **"Sequence matters more than scope."** — Adding nominalizers before passives, conditionals before causatives, ensures each new pattern builds on the previous one. A monolithic 13-sentence dump loses this scaffolding.

2. **"Three competing passives are two too many."** — The modern dataset already teaches 被. Adding 為…所… and 見-passive simultaneously creates confusion. Phase them.

3. **"Source diversity is content diversity."** — Each new source brings not just different vocabulary but a different *mode of argument*. 《莊子》 teaches through parables; 《戰國策》 through persuasive rhetoric; 《史記》 through narrative. The tree shows the same underlying structure across all three, which is the whole point.

4. **"Every new category needs a modern echo."** — Classical Conditionals ↔ modern 如果…就…; Classical Causatives ↔ modern 兼語句; Classical Passive ↔ modern 被字句. The Grammar Guide should explicitly reference these parallels.
