# Nomenclature Critique: Nested Structures & FLS

**Author**: Linguistics Specialist
**Task**: Theoretical Review of Nested Nomenclature
**Status**: Completed

The Orchestrator has proposed three new structural wrappers to solve the FLS root-constraint violations found in complex sentences: `Compound Sentence`, `Clause`, and `Object Clause`.

Here is my linguistic evaluation of these terms against the **Fractal Logic Stream (FLS)** framework and our pedagogical goals.

## 1. `Compound Sentence` vs. `Parallel Sentence`
- **FLS Conflict**: Low. "Compound Sentence" is a standard structural term, but it carries baggage from "Cathedral" (Indo-European) grammar implying specific coordinative conjunctions (and, but, or). FLS describes Mandarin as a "Train" architecture characterized by paratactic sequences.
- **Pedagogical View**: `Compound Sentence` is recognizable to learners. However, because FLS specifically describes these structures as "Parallel Transitions" (independent units subscribing to their own anchors), the term **`Parallel Sentence`** or **`Parallel Clauses`** might be more descriptive for learners to understand *why* the two halves sit side-by-side without an "and".
- **Verdict**: Acceptable, but **`Parallel Sentence`** is recommended as a slightly better fit for Mandarin's paratactic nature.

## 2. `Clause` vs. `Embedded Clause`
- **FLS Conflict**: Medium. A bare "Clause" lacks context. According to the FLS Matryoshka Principle, every Comment is a recursive container holding a *Nested Sentence Structure*. Calling it just a "Clause" minimizes the fact that it is a structurally complete mini-sentence nested deep in the tree.
- **Pedagogical View**: "Clause" is too generic and is often confused with "Phrase" by beginners. Adding "Embedded" clarifies the spatial relationship in the tree.
- **Verdict**: Reject `Clause` in favor of **`Embedded Clause`** or **`Nested Sentence`**. This perfectly captures FLS recursion while remaining entirely clear to a non-linguist.

## 3. `Object Clause` vs. `Situation Object`
- **FLS Conflict**: High (Terminologically). Section 7.2 of the FLS framework ("Pivotal Constructions") explicitly conceptualizes these as **Situation Objects**. It states: *The causative verb does not act directly on a person, but on a Situation.* 
- **Pedagogical View**: "Object Clause" is standard English grammar, but "Situation Object" beautifully explains to a learner *what* is happening. "The teacher made [the students answer a question]" -> The object is the entire *situation* of the students answering. However, if "Situation Object" sounds too academic, **`Embedded Clause`** can serve double duty here just fine.
- **Verdict**: Reject `Object Clause`. Use **`Situation Object`** if you want to lean fully into the FLS paradigm, or simply reuse **`Embedded Clause`** (with a sub-role of `object`) to keep the vocabulary list small and learner-friendly.

---

### Final Recommendation for the Data Linguist
Stick to a minimalist terminology set that maximizes spatial/structural clarity:
1. `Parallel Sentence` (for coordinated independent root sentences, e.g., 困难让人成长，安逸让人退步).
2. `Embedded Clause` (for any nested Topic-Comment structure, including pivotal objects and object clauses like 我觉得【你的想法很有道理】). 

This keeps the glossary tight, aligns with FLS recursion, and avoids Eurocentric grammatical baggage.
